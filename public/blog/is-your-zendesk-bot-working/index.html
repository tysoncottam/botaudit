Is your Zendesk bot actually working? Here's how to find out

March 2025 · 5 min read

You spent weeks configuring your Zendesk bot. You connected it to your knowledge base, wrote article mappings, and tested it yourself a few times. It seemed fine. So you went live.

Six months later, a customer tells you on a review site that your support bot "just keeps repeating itself and never actually helps." Sound familiar?

The problem is that most teams test their bot once — manually, by themselves — and then never test it again. Your customers are running thousands of conversations a month. You've run maybe ten.

What a Zendesk bot can silently fail at

Zendesk Messaging bots fail in ways that aren't obvious unless you're systematically testing them:

  • Inconsistent answers — The same question asked twice gets different answers. This happens when multiple articles match a query and the bot picks differently each time.
  • No response at all — The bot opens fine, you send a message, and… nothing. This is more common than you'd think, especially after knowledge base updates.
  • Slow responses — The bot technically responds but takes 12 seconds. Users have already rage-quit.
  • Wrong escalation path — When a customer says "I need a human," the bot should offer to connect them. Sometimes it doesn't.
  • Graceless failures — What happens when a customer sends gibberish? A well-configured bot says "I didn't understand that." A poorly configured one throws a generic error or loops.

How to test your Zendesk bot systematically

The only way to catch these problems reliably is to send the same questions multiple times and compare the answers. Here's a simple process you can run manually:

  1. Write a list of 10–20 questions that real customers ask. Pull from your ticket history if you have it.
  2. Open your support page in an incognito window (fresh session = no memory from prior conversations).
  3. Ask each question and record the response.
  4. Close the window, open a new incognito session, and ask each question again.
  5. Repeat three times and compare.

If you find inconsistencies, go back to your knowledge base and look for overlapping article coverage — two articles matching the same query is the most common cause.

What to look for in the responses

When you're reviewing responses, check for:

  • Do the answers match your actual policy? (Refund windows, escalation procedures, etc.)
  • Are the responses consistent across runs?
  • Does the bot offer a human handoff when asked?
  • Are response times under 6 seconds?
  • Does the bot recover gracefully from unclear or nonsense messages?
